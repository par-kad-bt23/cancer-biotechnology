⚕️ Biotechnology-Based Cancer Therapies


Targeted drug therapy involves using drugs that specifically target molecules involved in cancer cell growth, division, or survival, without affecting normal healthy cells.

  • Targets cancer-specific proteins or signaling pathways (e.g., HER2, tyrosine kinases)
  • Interferes with proliferation, angiogenesis, or survival signals
  • Reduces side effects compared to conventional chemotherapy

Examples: Trastuzumab for breast cancer, Imatinib for chronic myeloid leukemia

Immunotherapy uses the body’s immune system to recognize and destroy cancer cells.

  • Checkpoint inhibitors: Block PD-1/PD-L1 or CTLA-4 to activate T-cells
  • CAR-T cell therapy: Patient’s T-cells are genetically engineered to attack cancer cells
  • Cancer vaccines: Stimulate immune response against tumor antigens
  • Cytokine therapy: Uses interleukins or interferons to boost immunity

Effective for melanoma, lung cancer, lymphoma, and leukemia

Gene therapy involves introducing, removing, or modifying genes within cancer cells to treat disease.

  • Replace defective tumor suppressor genes (e.g., p53)
  • Introduce genes that induce apoptosis in cancer cells
  • Deliver genes that enhance sensitivity to chemo or radiation
  • Use viral vectors for targeted gene delivery

Experimental therapy for solid tumors and hematologic cancers

Cancer vaccines stimulate the immune system to recognize and attack cancer cells.

  • Preventive vaccines: Target viruses that cause cancer (e.g., HPV vaccine)
  • Therapeutic vaccines: Stimulate immune response against existing tumors

Examples: Sipuleucel-T for prostate cancer

Personalized medicine tailors cancer treatment based on an individual patient’s genetic, molecular, and cellular profile.

  • Genomic sequencing to identify mutations and actionable targets
  • Selects therapies most effective for the patient’s cancer type
  • Monitors response to adjust treatment dynamically

Maximizes effectiveness, reduces toxicity, and improves survival
