Cancer is classified based on the type of cell or tissue from which it originates. The major types of cancer are explained below:

Carcinoma is the most common type of cancer and originates from epithelial cells, which form the lining of internal organs and the outer surface of the skin.

These cells are frequently exposed to environmental factors such as chemicals, radiation, and pathogens, making them more prone to mutations. Carcinomas often grow slowly initially but may invade surrounding tissues and spread (metastasize) through the lymphatic system.

  • Affects skin, lungs, breast, colon, prostate
  • Spreads mainly through lymph nodes
  • Examples: Breast cancer, Lung cancer, Colorectal cancer

Sarcoma is a rare type of cancer that arises from mesenchymal or connective tissues such as bone, cartilage, muscle, fat, and blood vessels.

Sarcomas tend to occur deeper within the body and may grow to a large size before detection. Unlike carcinomas, they usually spread through the bloodstream rather than lymphatic channels.

  • Occurs in arms, legs, chest, or abdomen
  • More aggressive in younger individuals
  • Examples: Osteosarcoma, Liposarcoma

3Ô∏è‚É£ Leukemia

Leukemia is a cancer of blood-forming tissues, particularly the bone marrow, resulting in abnormal and excessive production of white blood cells.

These abnormal cells interfere with normal blood cell production, leading to anemia, infections, and bleeding disorders. Leukemia does not form solid tumors and spreads throughout the bloodstream.

  • Impairs immune system function
  • Causes fatigue, infections, and bleeding
  • Types: Acute leukemia, Chronic leukemia

Lymphoma is a cancer of the lymphatic system, which plays a crucial role in immunity by transporting lymph and immune cells.

It typically begins in lymph nodes and can spread to the spleen, liver, bone marrow, and other organs. Swollen, painless lymph nodes are a common early symptom.

  • Affects lymph nodes, spleen, thymus

Myeloma is a cancer of plasma cells, specialized white blood cells responsible for antibody production.

The abnormal plasma cells accumulate in bone marrow, weakening bones and suppressing normal immune function. Patients often suffer from bone pain, fractures, and recurrent infections.

  • Reduces antibody production
  • Causes bone damage and anemia
  • Also called Multiple Myeloma
